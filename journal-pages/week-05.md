**Thematic Focus and Data Source(s)**

This project would explore micro acts of care, small everyday gestures such as checking on a friend, sharing resources, watering plants or helping with tasks. These actions are socially essential but yet they are typically invisible and unnoticed.
This project is set in the plausible future where the cities have implemented a system in which each act of care earns ‘care minutes’ that can be exchanged for services or community benefits. As the system becomes more normalised and people begin tracking and visualizing care contributions.
The data may be self collected by logging my own care acts, crowdsourced from a small group and or simulated from citizens in the future. The data is qualitative, small scaled and static and it consists of notes describing each act of the care.
This data would be translated into a 3d paper garden where each leaf represents an act of care encoded by time, emotion and the type of support 
This project aligns with the data representation of data humanism by prioritizing empathy, storytelling and embodied representation over the efficiency as it draws on qualitative visualisation and slow data principles creating an tactile dashboard that restores emotional meaning to data. 

**Visualisation & Impact**

The project will take the form of a 3d paper garden that will be a tactile approach similar to the data portrait done in week one but instead of the abstract charts but the visualisation becomes physical from folded,cut and layer paper, with each leaf representing acts of care.
How this would work is i will do crowdsourcing from a small group collecting data to make the paper garden. 
The provocation of this project is that care is an essential part of society but it still remains invisible and undervalued. And by imagining a future where the cities award care minutes for the everyday acts and the project challenges the audiences to question what happens when acts become measured. 
I want the audience to take a moment in noticing and paying attention to all the different small details that would usually slip past them. I want the audience to see everyday care differently, to recognise the weight behind the acts of care that give and receive and considering their own patterns of care they do.
